She was born on November 7, 1867, in Warsaw, Poland, and lived until July 4, 1934. Curie is best known for her discovery of the radioactive elements polonium and radium, as well as her pioneering research on radioactivity. Alongside her husband, Pierre Curie, she conducted significant experiments that led to the development of the concept of "radioactivity" and the isolation of radioactive substances. In recognition of her groundbreaking work, Marie Curie became the first woman to win a Nobel Prize. She was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1903, which she shared with her husband and Antoine Henri Becquerel. Just a few years later, in 1911, she received her second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her discovery of radium and polonium. Marie Curie's research not only revolutionized the field of physics and chemistry, but it also laid the foundation for modern advancements in medicine and technology. Her contributions to the understanding of radioactivity opened up new avenues for nuclear science and paved the way for the development of radiation therapy for cancer treatment. Despite facing numerous barriers as a woman in science during her time, Marie Curie's dedication, perseverance, and intellectual brilliance shattered gender norms and inspired generations of future scientists. She remains an iconic figure in the scientific community and a powerful symbol of female empowerment and achievement.
